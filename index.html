How attribution works

01 / DISCLOSE

Start with the filing

Every entry begins from a public source — an SEC filing or press release stating a SOL acquisition, a custody arrangement, or a staking deal, with dates and amounts.

02 / TRACE

Follow the chain

On-chain flows are matched to the disclosure: funding timing, exchange inflows (FalconX, Coinbase Prime), validator staking, and custody controllers — until the wallets line up.

03 / RATE

Label the confidence

Nothing is presented as absolute. Each wallet carries a confidence level — verified, high, or publicly labeled — so you can judge the evidence yourself.

Methodology — the techniques, in the open

no black boxes · reproduce every step yourself

Wallet attribution can look like magic. It isn't. It's a repeatable chain of evidence anyone can follow with a block explorer and patience. Here's the exact method behind every entry on this page — so you can check our work, or run your own.

01

Anchor on a disclosure

Every trace starts off-chain. An SEC 8-K, an 8-K/A, or a press release that states a SOL purchase, a custody arrangement, or a staking deal — with a date and an amount. That gives you a when and a how much to hunt for on the chain.

Example — "Helius (HSDT) begins purchasing SOL, 760,190 SOL" on 2025-09-22. Now you know the rough size and window to look for.
02

Match the funding flow

Find on-chain inflows whose timing and size line up with the disclosure. A treasury buying hundreds of thousands of SOL leaves a footprint: large transfers out of an exchange or OTC desk (Coinbase Prime, FalconX) into fresh addresses, clustered around the announced date.

Signal — a 1M+ SOL transfer arriving directly from a Solana Foundation non-circulating supply wallet is about as strong as on-chain evidence gets.
03

Fingerprint the custodian

Custodians leave recognizable on-chain patterns — deposit-address structures, program interactions, and stake-authority layouts that repeat across their clients. Matching those patterns tells you who controls the keys, not just where the coins sit.

Tells — Coinbase Prime, Fireblocks, Anchorage and BitGo each route and stake differently. The plumbing gives them away.
04

Cluster the stake & deposit accounts

Follow each stake account back to its stake authority and withdraw authority; group deposit addresses that share a controller. One confirmed wallet expands into the full set this way — custody wallets, stake accounts, and the validators they delegate to.

Method — shared authorities and repeated counterparties turn a single address into a mapped treasury.
05

Cross-validate before you label

Never publish on a single signal. Require at least two independent lines — say, a timing match plus a custodian fingerprint plus a corroborating filing — before attributing an address. Then assign a confidence level, not a verdict.

Rule — if it only checks one box, it stays in your notes, not on the registry.
Verified

A definitive on-chain link — a direct transfer from a known entity or Foundation wallet, or a transaction the entity itself signed. The strongest tier.

High confidence

Strong evidentiary clustering — timing, size, custodian fingerprint and disclosure all converge — but no single cryptographic proof of ownership.

Publicly labeled

The entity or its custodian has publicly disclosed the address. We relay it and run an independent sanity check, but the source is their statement.

Where this method stops. Clustering is probabilistic. "High confidence" means the evidence strongly converges — not that ownership is legally proven. Every attribution here can be challenged with better evidence, and that's the point of doing it in the open: a wrong call is visible and fixable, not buried in a private dataset.
